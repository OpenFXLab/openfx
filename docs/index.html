FX Positioning and
Macro Intelligence

OpenFXLab is a planned open-source platform that combines CFTC foreign exchange positioning data with macroeconomic context — helping traders, researchers, and developers understand how currency markets are positioned and whether the macro environment supports those positions.

View on GitHub Read the Brief

The problem

Currency researchers currently piece together information from many separate places: CFTC reports, central bank publications, national statistics agencies, and financial data feeds. The underlying information is largely publicly available, although licensing, attribution, and redistribution requirements vary by source. The work of collecting it, normalizing it, and combining it is repetitive, error-prone, and time-consuming.

Simple COT-charting websites display raw positioning but lack macro context. Institutional terminals provide sophisticated analytics but are expensive and closed. The gap between those two options — more analytical than a chart site, more transparent and accessible than a professional terminal — is the space OpenFXLab aims to serve.

Fragmented sources

Positioning data, central bank rates, inflation figures, and price history all live in separate systems with different formats and update schedules.

No historical context

Raw positioning numbers are difficult to interpret without knowing whether a level is historically large, small, or typical.

Missing macro connection

A position that made sense three months ago may be vulnerable today if the rate differential has shifted — but simple tools don't show that.

The proposed solution

A modular open-source platform that ingests and normalizes CFTC positioning data, calculates standard analytical metrics, connects positioning with macro drivers, and exposes everything through a web interface, API, and data downloads — with every formula and data source documented openly.

▪
Positioning data — Net positions, weekly changes, historical percentiles, and z-scores for EUR, JPY, GBP, CAD, AUD, CHF, and NZD. Separate breakdowns for leveraged funds, asset managers, and dealers.
▪
Macro context — Central bank policy rates, interest rate differentials, inflation, employment, and growth indicators alongside positioning data.
▪
Analytical indicators — Crowding assessment, positioning-price divergence detection, and positioning-macro divergence flags.
▪
Open methodology — Every formula documented and reproducible. Every data point attributed to its source with freshness timestamps.
▪
API and downloads — RESTful JSON API and CSV downloads for programmatic access. Python package planned for Phase 4.

Example: JPY positioning scenario

Illustrative example — not live market data or investment advice

Leveraged funds hold a historically large net short position in JPY futures (positioning percentile: 8th — extremely net short). The short position has grown for five consecutive weeks. However, USD/JPY has stopped making new highs despite the remaining speculative short. Meanwhile, the U.S.–Japan interest rate differential has begun narrowing as the Bank of Japan signals a shift toward tighter policy.

Research observation: The speculative short position is historically crowded and is not being confirmed by recent price behavior. The macro conditions that supported the short are weakening. This combination may represent an increased risk of a rapid short unwind.

This is the type of context OpenFXLab aims to surface — as a research input, not as a trading signal or forecast.

Why open source

Transparent methodology

Financial data tools often obscure their calculations. OpenFXLab takes the opposite approach: every formula is documented, every data source is cited, and every calculation is reproducible from the published code. Users can verify any number independently.

Accessible infrastructure

Students, independent researchers, and small funds cannot afford institutional terminal subscriptions. Open-source development lowers the barrier to high-quality FX positioning analysis for users who need more than a basic chart but less than a full institutional platform.

The core code and methodology are released under the MIT License. A future hosted service may add convenience features, but the analytical engine remains open. See open-source model.
